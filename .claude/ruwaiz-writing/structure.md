# Structure

Ruwaiz uses a loose adaptation of the NYT essay arc, scaled to format. The arc is invisible scaffolding — the voice wraps around it.

## The arc

**Hook → Context → Parallel → Turn → Argument → Reflection**

It's not a checklist. It's a skeleton. Some pieces compress it; some expand it. But every Ruwaiz piece worth reading has at least three of these, and one of them is always the **turn**.

### 1. Hook

Open in a moment, a tension, or a question. Never a thesis statement.

What this looks like in his writing:

- **A scene**: "It began in 2009, when a new classmate mentioned he built websites." (Blogspot post)
- **A tension**: "With all the *Winter Arc* conversations floating around on social media, December is often portrayed as a month of darkness, cold, and decline. But for me, December has always felt different." (December)
- **A question**: "How many times in your life have you wondered seeing the alternative possibilities of a particular product?" (Unintended Consequences)
- **A confession**: "I love this game 🥸" (CODM)

What it doesn't look like:

- "Design is about empathy."
- "In this post, I'll explore..."
- "There's been a lot of conversation about X lately."

### 2. Context

Why does this matter to him, right now? Frame it. Personal, historical, or industry context — whichever gives the piece weight.

This is usually short. One paragraph. Sometimes one sentence.

> "Until that moment, the web felt distant, something built by extra ordinary people, not someone like me."

That single line is doing all the framing work for the Blogspot post.

### 3. Parallel / setup

What's similar, what's familiar, what the reader (or he, in earlier life) already believed. This sets up the turn.

In the AI Tools post, the parallel is: he tried Trae, code appeared, things worked. This is the "easy read" — AI is magic.

In the Spider-Man section of June, the parallel is: he's always been a PC gamer, console exclusivity feels unnecessary.

### 4. Turn (the engine)

This is the most important part. Every piece pivots on a "but," "except," "and yet," or a quiet realization.

Examples from his writing:

> "But playing Spider-Man 2 on the PS5 made me pause a bit."

> "But I started noticing the friction. Every new post meant opening a full HTML file."

> "But it came with baggage: databases, backends, admin panels, things breaking for reasons I didn't understand."

> "Until they didn't."

> "But after a while comes THE VENOM, and good lord what an amazing sight it was."

If you can't articulate the turn before drafting, the piece isn't ready. Surface this to Ruwaiz before writing — "what's the turn here?" — if it isn't already obvious from the conversation.

### 5. Argument

What does he actually think? This is where description becomes argument. Not a corporate "key insight" — a real one, often slightly contrarian, often hedged.

> "Maybe it isn't just about gatekeeping. Maybe it's about building something deeply optimized, where the hardware and software are fully in sync."

> "We often mistake unfamiliarity for incompatibility."

The argument is usually one or two sentences, embedded in the flow. He doesn't build to it like a thesis — he just lets it surface.

### 6. Reflection

Don't summarize. Land somewhere.

A reflection is:

- Specific (not "this taught me a lot")
- Slightly open (an implication, a question, a quiet warning)
- Earned by what came before

Examples:

> "December reminded me that growth doesn't always need to be loud. Its the consistency that compounds to be the behemoth."

> "When structure is done right, you stop noticing it. And you finally get to focus on what you wanted to do in the first place, a concept I carry into design every day."

> "That's enough."

What a reflection is *not*:

- "And that's why this matters."
- "In summary, X, Y, and Z."
- "I'm grateful for the journey."

## How the arc bends per format

### Live notes

Loose. The arc shows up across the whole month, not within each section. Each section can be its own micro-arc (hook, observation, small turn, small reflection) and the whole post lands with one bigger reflection at the end.

The November and December posts are good examples. Each subsection (Health, SwiftUI, Games) has a tiny arc. The full post lands on a single reflective beat.

### Essays

The arc does heavier lifting. The turn is more pronounced. The argument is more developed. Length is longer (1500–4000 words). But — still — never academic, still hedged, still ends on reflection not summary.

The CODM analysis is the most structured essay in the corpus. Even there, the opening is a hook ("I love this game"), the body builds context and parallels (motivations, archetypes, models), the turn is implicit (most games fail at this; CODM doesn't), the argument is the analysis itself, and the reflection is the closing sensor tower line — quiet, not triumphant.

### Series posts

Episodic. The arc applies to each post, but each post also nods backwards and forwards. The hook often references where the previous post left off. The reflection often hints at the next one.

> "In the next part, I'll write about what happened when publishing became effortless..."

This is the pattern. Don't end series posts with summary — end with the next door opening.

### Gaming UX articles (analytical essays)

The most rigorous register. The arc is:

- **Hook**: a tension in the design space (e.g., "how do games guide players without breaking flow?")
- **Context**: framework or industry context (Kahneman, Csikszentmihalyi, retention models)
- **Parallel**: what most games do
- **Turn**: but here's where it breaks down / here's what most games miss
- **Argument**: his original contribution (e.g., "error-informed nudges")
- **Reflection**: implication for practitioners; honest acknowledgment of what's unsolved

This is more "professional" but the voice still survives. Keep the asides. Keep the undercuts.

## Common structural failures to watch for

**Missing turn.** Piece is all observation, no pivot. Reads as a journal entry, not a published post. Add a "but" somewhere in the middle third.

**Stacked turns.** Three "buts" in three paragraphs. The piece loses its spine. Pick one real turn; the others are probably just transitions.

**Premature argument.** Stating the thesis in paragraph one. Cut it; let it surface in paragraph four or five.

**Wrap-up reflection.** Closing with a summary instead of a reflection. Replace with something quieter, more specific, or more open.

**No anchor.** No concrete moment, name, tool, or memory. Reads as abstract. Ask Ruwaiz for a specific.
