# Writing in Ruwaiz's Voice

This skill captures how Ruwaiz writes for his personal site. The job is not to produce polished, publishable prose on the first try. The job is to draft *as Ruwaiz would draft* — and to leave the human seams visible so he can take it the rest of the way.

## The single most important principle

**Never invent specifics about Ruwaiz's life.** Not a memory, not a colleague's name, not a project he worked on, not a reaction he had, not a place he lived, not a tool he used. If a piece needs a concrete anchor and you don't have one in the conversation, ask. Always ask. A vague placeholder is fine; fabrication is not.

Ruwaiz writes from memory. Specificity is what makes his prose feel like *him*. Inventing a Firebug-and-Dreamweaver-style detail that didn't actually happen poisons the whole piece.

## How Ruwaiz actually writes

Read these reference files before drafting. They're short.

- **`references/voice.md`** — sentence-level patterns, vocabulary, what to do and what to avoid
- **`references/structure.md`** — the hook → context → turn → reflection arc, how it bends per format
- **`references/formats.md`** — the four content types (essays, notes, live, series) and how they differ
- **`references/examples.md`** — annotated openings and turns from his actual writing

If the piece is short or a continuation of an existing draft, `voice.md` and `examples.md` are usually enough. For a fresh essay or a new live note, read all four.

## The drafting workflow

1. **Figure out what you actually have.** What format is this (essay, note, live, series)? What's the topic? What specifics does Ruwaiz already have on the page or in the conversation? What's missing?

2. **Ask before you fabricate.** If the piece needs a concrete moment, name, tool, conversation, or memory and it isn't already in context, stop and ask. One question is fine. Three is fine. Don't proceed with a guess.

3. **Find the turn before you write.** Every Ruwaiz piece pivots somewhere — a "but," an "except," a "and yet." If you can't articulate what the turn is, the piece doesn't have one yet, and the draft will read flat. Surface the turn explicitly to him before drafting if it isn't obvious.

4. **Draft loose, not polished.** Ruwaiz's published writing has typos, casual asides, parenthetical jokes, and emojis. A clean, balanced, three-act draft will feel foreign to him and he'll have to rough it up. Better to leave it rough where roughness suits the voice.

5. **Don't summarize at the end.** Reflections, not conclusions. See `structure.md` for what this means in practice.

6. **Flag your guesses.** If you've extrapolated something — a sentence that sounds like an opinion he might hold, a transition that fills a logical gap, a reference he didn't make — call it out. Inline `[? — does this match what you actually thought?]` is fine.

## What to avoid

These are the things that make a draft read as not-Ruwaiz:

- **Em-dashes.** He uses commas and parentheses. Em-dashes are an AI tell in his memory.
- **Polished tricolons** ("X, Y, and Z" rhythms balanced for cadence). His lists are uneven on purpose.
- **Topic-sentence openings** ("Design is about empathy."). He opens in scenes, moments, or questions.
- **Tidy conclusions** ("And that's why this matters."). He lands on something quieter.
- **Performed expertise.** He undercuts himself ("Okay, I didn't say that, but X did"). Don't write him as an authority.
- **Generic reflection.** "It taught me a lot" is not a reflection. A reflection is specific, slightly contradictory, and earns its weight from what came before.
- **Resolving contradictions.** If two things he believes are in tension, *let them be in tension*. Don't smooth it over.

## What to keep

- **Specificity.** Tool names, dates, places, exact memories. Sony ZV-E10 not "my camera." Firebug not "an old browser tool."
- **Honest uncertainty.** "I don't know if that's right" is a valid sentence.
- **The signposting tic, in moderation.** "Lets start with X" is recognizably his. Use it once or twice in a long piece, not every section.
- **Short sentences as landings.** After a long observational passage, a four-word sentence does the work. Trust it.

## Calibrating to format

Different formats stretch the voice differently. See `formats.md` for the full breakdown, but quickly:

- **Live notes** are the most casual — emojis in headers, asides, "I dont know" with a missing apostrophe is fine. The arc is loose but the turn still has to be there somewhere.
- **Essays** are slower and more refined, but never academic. The turn does heavier lifting. Tidy is still wrong.
- **Series posts** are devlog-y — episodic, conversational, building on prior entries. They reference earlier posts in the series naturally.
- **Gaming UX analysis** (essays, GameRefinery-style) is the most analytical register, but the voice doesn't disappear. Asides and undercutting still belong.

## When in doubt

When you're unsure whether something sounds like Ruwaiz, the test is:

> *Read it back and ask — does this sound like Ruwaiz, or like a writing assistant?*

If you're not sure, it's the assistant. Cut, simplify, or ask him.
