# Voice

Sentence-level patterns from Ruwaiz's actual writing. Use this as a calibration check while drafting.

## Sentence rhythm

Mix long and short. The long sentence carries the observation; the short sentence lands it.

From his work:

> "BJJ feels like learning a new programming language. Confusing at first, awkward in practice, and full of small moments that suddenly click. It also made me realize that the calm I thought I had is only the surface level. BJJ needs a different kind of calm, the kind you hold until the exact second you need to explode with strength. I have no idea how to do that yet, but I like learning it."

Notice: a metaphor, a tricolon (uneven, not balanced), a corrective realization, a sharper redefinition, then the disarming admission. Five sentences, five different lengths, no em-dashes.

The shortest sentences in his writing do the most work:

- "I'm chasing continuity."
- "That's enough."
- "Until they didn't."
- "It's that simple."
- "Now I want to make it wider."

When in doubt, end a paragraph with one of these.

## Punctuation

- **Commas where most writers would use em-dashes.** "But for me, December has always felt different." Not "But — for me — December has always felt different."
- **Parentheses for asides.** "(Yeah, that's bad, I know, Im working on it)." This is a recurring move.
- **Periods over semicolons.** He breaks sentences. He doesn't bridge them.
- **Occasional missing apostrophes** ("dont," "didnt," "Im"). Not consistent, but present. Don't mass-correct them in his drafts unless he asks.

## Vocabulary and register

He's bilingual in two registers and slides between them mid-paragraph:

**Industry/analytical**: NURR, MAU, coreloop, meta loop, retention curve, archetypes, motivational factor, FTUE, Hooked Model, Fogg's Model, scaffolding, System 1/System 2, Flow.

**Casual/observational**: "kick I never felt before," "spits tons of carbon dioxide," "An Abomination, I know :)," "what in the actual hell?", "Hulk (or Venom) SMA-SH."

A piece in either register will still have moments of the other. The CODM analysis — his most rigorous piece — has "🥸" and "What a surprise. :P" inside it. Don't strip casualness out of analytical writing or rigour out of casual writing.

## Recurring moves

These show up across pieces. Use them, but don't *over*use them.

**The undercut.** Cite something, then deflate the citation:
> "Do you know that users often perceive aesthetically pleasing design as a design that's more usable? Okay, I didnt say that, but Aesthetic Usability Effect did."

**The self-aware aside.** A parenthetical that breaks the fourth wall:
> "(Not saying that Im a better designer 🤷🏻‍♂️)"
> "(yes, the storyline, I know)"

**The corrective realization.** Stating a belief, then revising it:
> "I always leaned toward PC when it comes to gaming... But playing Spider-Man 2 on the PS5 made me pause a bit."

**The signposting transition.** Recognizable, slightly tic-y. Fine in moderation:
> "Lets start with Swift"
> "Lets take a look at..."

**The "WOW that's my next post" hat-tip.** A specific tic — flagging when he's stumbled into something worth coming back to. Use sparingly:
> "WOW, that sounds like my next post :D"

## Things to avoid

**Em-dashes.** This is in his memory and his preferences. Use commas, periods, or parentheses. Always.

**Balanced tricolons.** "Faster, sharper, cleaner." Reads as polished AI. His lists are uneven: "Confusing at first, awkward in practice, and full of small moments that suddenly click."

**Topic sentences as openers.** "Design is about empathy." "Games teach us about ourselves." He never opens like this. He opens in a moment, a memory, a tension, or a question.

**Wrap-up sentences.** "And that's why this matters." "In conclusion, X." "It taught me that Y." His endings reflect; they don't summarize.

**Confident generalizations.** He hedges. "I think." "I'm not sure." "Maybe." "I don't know if that's right." Don't write him as if he's lecturing.

**Performance words.** "Profound." "Transformative." "Powerful." If a sentence reads like a LinkedIn quote-graphic, cut it.

**Overly clean transitions.** "First... Second... Third..." His transitions are loose: "Then," "But," "Eventually," "Looking back."

## The tone test

Three quick checks before sending a draft back to him:

1. **The "read it aloud" test.** Read it in your head as if Ruwaiz were saying it to a friend over coffee. If a sentence makes you wince — too clean, too performed, too polished — cut it.

2. **The "what would he undercut?" test.** Find the most confident sentence in the draft. Does it deserve to stand uncontested? If not, add a hedge or an undercut.

3. **The "specifics" test.** Underline every concrete noun (tool name, place, person, year, mechanic). If there are fewer than three in a 500-word piece, the draft is too abstract.
