# Examples

Annotated passages from Ruwaiz's actual writing. Use these as calibration when drafting. Pattern-match against the real thing, not against generic "good writing."

## Openings

### Live note (December)

> "With all the *Winter Arc* conversations floating around on social media, December is often portrayed as a month of darkness, cold, and decline. But for me, December has always felt different. It feels calm. A month for slowing down, reflecting, and quietly collecting fuel for what comes next."

**Why it works**: Opens with a cultural reference (Winter Arc), then immediately turns against it ("But for me"). The first sentence is long and observational; the second is the pivot; the third is short and lands ("It feels calm."); the fourth is uneven and slightly poetic. Five sentences, four rhythms, one clear turn already established.

### Essay (Unintended Consequences)

> "How many times in your life have you wondered seeing the alternative possibilities of a particular product?"

**Why it works**: Opens with a question to the reader. Doesn't announce the topic. Pulls the reader into a moment of self-reflection before the argument starts.

### Series (Blogspot: The first patch of soil)

> "It began in 2009, when a new classmate mentioned he built websites. Until that moment, the web felt distant, something built by extra ordinary people, not someone like me."

**Why it works**: Specific date, specific moment, specific person. The second sentence does all the framing in one line — establishing both the protagonist's prior worldview and the implicit promise that it's about to change. No thesis statement.

### Note (32)

> "I turned 32 today.\n\nIt doesn't feel dramatic, but a quiet pause in the year.\nA moment to check in with myself and notice how I've been moving through life."

**Why it works**: A flat declarative opener, then a soft contradiction ("doesn't feel dramatic, but..."). The line breaks slow the reader down. He's setting the pace of the whole piece in three lines.

### Analytical essay (CODM)

> "I love this game 🥸 (As if its not apparent, already)"

**Why it works**: An analytical piece that opens with a confession, an emoji, and a parenthetical that breaks the fourth wall. Establishes early that this is going to be rigorous *and* personal. Don't strip the personal voice out of analytical writing.

## Turns

### From AI Tools as Creative Partners

> "Using Trae was like rubbing Aladdin's lamp. Code appeared, things worked, but I didn't understand any of it. It was like having a wish granted without knowing the cost."
>
> **"That's when it clicked: if I kept going down this road, I'd never really *learn* anything."**

**Why it works**: Sets up the parallel (AI as magic), then turns explicitly with "That's when it clicked." The italicized "learn" carries the argument. Not preachy.

### From Jekyll and the Static Web

> "I tried self-hosted WordPress for a bit. It worked. But it came with baggage: databases, backends, admin panels, things breaking for reasons I didn't understand. All I wanted to do was write and publish. Nothing more."

**Why it works**: The turn is "But it came with baggage." The list of frustrations is uneven and specific. The closing two sentences land the argument by stripping it down. "Nothing more." is the period the whole paragraph was building toward.

### From June (Spider-Man section)

> "I've always leaned toward PC when it comes to gaming. More control, better performance, ability to mod things the way I like. Console exclusivity always felt unnecessary to me."
>
> **"But playing Spider-Man 2 on the PS5 made me pause a bit."**

**Why it works**: Builds the parallel (PC supremacist) over three sentences, then breaks it with one. The pause-a-bit framing is humble — he doesn't claim a full conversion, just a moment of doubt.

## Reflections (closings)

### Live note (December)

> "December reminded me that growth doesn't always need to be loud. Its the consistency that compounds to be the behemoth."

**Why it works**: Specific to the month. Slightly metaphorical ("compounds to be the behemoth") in a way that's a little awkward — and that's fine, it sounds like him talking, not like a polished aphorism. Doesn't summarise; lands.

### Note (32)

> "So here's my reminder for the year ahead:\n- Doesnt have to outrun anything.\n- Compounding is the magic I need to strive for.\n- Stay curious, choose what feels true, and let the rest unfold at its own pace.\n\nThat's enough."

**Why it works**: A list (not balanced, slightly ungrammatical). Then "That's enough." — a two-word landing that does more work than any wrap-up paragraph could.

### Series (Jekyll)

> "When structure is done right, you stop noticing it. And you finally get to focus on what you wanted to do in the first place, a concept I carry into design every day.\n\nIn the next part, I'll write about what happened when publishing became effortless, and how that changed my relationship with showing work."

**Why it works**: A reflection that names the principle ("when structure is done right, you stop noticing it") and connects it to design. Then opens the next door without closing the current one. Series posts always do this.

## Asides and undercuts

These are sentences that wouldn't survive in polished prose but are *exactly* what makes his writing his.

> "(Yeah, that's bad, I know, Im working on it)"

> "(Not saying that Im a better designer 🤷🏻‍♂️)"

> "Okay, I didnt say that, but Aesthetic Usability Effect did."

> "WOW, that sounds like my next post :D"

> "yes, the storyline, I know"

> "(yes, Zynga sunsetted the game after 8 incredible years)"

> "Pay to win scenario arises when the user who spends more money with in game have an upper hand all the time apart from experience."

Notice: he hedges his own claims, breaks the fourth wall, and writes asides that read like spoken speech. **Don't strip these out when editing his drafts. Don't refuse to add them when generating drafts.**

## What to do when nothing in this file matches the situation

If you're stuck — the topic is unfamiliar, the format is hybrid, the brief is vague — go back to the basic test:

> *Read it back and ask — does this sound like Ruwaiz, or like a writing assistant?*

If you can't tell, ask him. One specific question is better than a fluent fabrication.
